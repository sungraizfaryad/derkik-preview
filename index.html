CHAPTER ONE

The Little Boat

Some islands cannot be found on any map.

They only appear when adventure is looking for you.

Danno believed that before he had ever seen one.

Joeby did not.

1

Joeby believed in toast, dry socks, and not being eaten by anything with teeth. Unfortunately, he also believed in his older brother, which was how most of his problems began.

“Slow down!” Joeby puffed, chasing Danno across the empty beach.

“You’re six,” Danno called back. “You’re supposed to be fast.”

“I’m nearly seven!”

“That’s worse. You should be faster.”

2

The tide had slipped away from Blackwater Cove, leaving the old jetty standing on crooked legs above wet sand and black pools. Fishing boats rocked lazily in the harbour. Gulls argued on the rooftops. The village still slept.

Danno stopped beneath the jetty.

Joeby bent over, hands on knees.

“If this is about crabs again,” he said, “I’m going home.”

Danno grinned.

“It’s not crabs.”

3

“That’s what you said last time.”

“Look.”

Behind a curtain of seaweed and rotten rope, something blue was wedged beneath the wooden beams.

Joeby crouched.

“Is that...a boat?”

Danno pulled away more seaweed.

A little blue dinghy lay half-buried in sand. Its paint was cracked. One side had split. Barnacles clung to it like old teeth.

“It’s perfect,” Danno whispered.

Joeby stared at him.

4

“It’s broken.”

“Exactly.”

“How is that perfect?”

“Because nobody will miss it.”

Joeby took one step backwards.

“No.”

“Yes.”

“No, Danno.”

“We could fix her.”

“We?”

“You’re my assistant.”

“I never agreed to that.”

“You came.”

“That’s not agreement. That’s being tricked.”

5

Danno laughed and ran his hand along the boat’s faded side. Something about it made his chest tighten. The dinghy looked forgotten, but not dead. Not quite.

Joeby peered around the jetty.

“Mum said we had to be back before lunch.”

“We will be.”

“You said that when we climbed the lighthouse.”

“We were nearly back before lunch.”

“We were back before tea.”

“That’s still before something.”

6

Joeby tried not to smile.

Danno grabbed a loose rope tied to the bow.

“Help me pull.”

“No.”

“Fine. I’ll tell everyone you were scared of a boat.”

Joeby’s face hardened.

“I am not scared of boats.”

“You’re scared of seaweed.”

“Seaweed grabs ankles.”

“It does not.”

“You don’t know what it does when you’re not looking.”

7

Danno laughed so loudly a gull flew from the jetty roof.

Together they pulled.

At first the boat didn’t move. Then, with a scrape that sounded much too loud in the quiet morning, it slid free.

Both boys froze.

No windows opened.

No fishermen shouted.

No mother appeared at the top of the beach with folded arms and that look.

Joeby breathed again.

“We should put it back.”

8

Danno looked at the boat.

Then the sea.

Then the horizon.

“What if we sailed to Pirate Rock?”

Joeby stared.

“That’s past the harbour.”

“Only a little.”

“It’s past where we’re allowed.”

“That’s why it’s called adventure.”

“No, that’s why it’s called trouble.”

9

Danno crouched beside the dinghy, already inspecting the split wood.

“She needs patching. New rope. Oars.”

“We haven’t got oars.”

“We can make some.”

“We can’t make oars.”

“We can try.”

Joeby sighed the long, tired sigh of someone who knew his future had just been ruined.

Danno looked up.

“You don’t have to come.”

That was unfair.

10

Danno knew it was unfair.

Joeby looked at the water shining beneath the morning sun. He looked at the little blue boat. Then he looked at his brother.

Someone had to keep Danno alive.

“I’m coming,” he said. “But if we die, I’m telling Mum it was your fault.”

Danno grinned.

“If we die, you won’t be able to.”

“I’ll find a way.”

11

All that day, they worked beneath the jetty in secret. Danno scraped barnacles with a flat stone. Joeby passed him the wrong bits of wood, dropped nails in the sand, and once got his sleeve stuck in tar.

“Hold it still,” Danno said.

“I am!”

“You’re wobbling it.”

“I’m not wobbling it.”

The boat wobbled.

Danno laughed.

“You’re the worst shipbuilder in Wales.”

12

Joeby picked something from the sand.

“I’m the best shell finder though.”

He held up a small striped shell, white and gold and smooth as glass.

Danno glanced over.

“Nice.”

“Can I keep it?”

“It’s a shell. You don’t need permission.”

Joeby slipped it carefully into his pocket.

13

By sunset, the dinghy looked less dead. Not good. Not safe, exactly. But ready enough for two boys who didn’t understand boats and didn’t want to.

Danno stood back, proud.

“Tomorrow,” he said, “we launch her.”

Joeby’s mouth opened.

“Tomorrow?”

Danno nodded.

“Early.”

“Without telling Mum?”

Danno smiled.

“Especially without telling Mum.”

14

Above them, the tide turned.

Out beyond the harbour wall, where the sea darkened towards the horizon, something moved beneath the water.

Not a fish.

Not a wave.

A current.

Silent.

Waiting.

15

CHAPTER TWO

The Launch

Danno woke before the gulls.

He was dressed before the kettle boiled downstairs, out of the house before the first floorboard creaked, and halfway to the beach before he realised Joeby was behind him.

“You’re loud,” Danno whispered.

“I’m breathing.”

“Breathe quieter.”

“I’m carrying bread.”

Danno stopped.

16

“You brought food?”

Joeby pulled half a loaf from under his jumper.

“And apples.”

Danno blinked.

“That’s actually useful.”

“I’m not useless.”

“I never said you were useless.”

“You called me the worst shipbuilder in Wales.”

“That was yesterday.”

Joeby sniffed.

“I’ve improved.”

17

The beach lay silver in the early light. The old jetty stretched into the water like a crooked finger. Beneath it, the little blue boat waited.

Danno’s heart thumped.

It suddenly looked smaller than it had yesterday.

Joeby noticed.

“It’s very little.”

“It’s big enough.”

“For what?”

“For us.”

“That’s not the same thing.”

18

They dragged the boat to the edge of the tide. It scraped over the sand, leaving a crooked trail behind them.

“Quiet!” Danno whispered.

“You’re the one scraping a boat across the beach.”

“Push.”

“I am pushing.”

“You’re leaning.”

“I’m thoughtfully pushing.”

A small wave curled around their ankles.

The dinghy lifted.

Joeby gasped.

19

“It’s floating.”

Danno tried not to look surprised.

“I told you it would.”

“You didn’t know.”

“I strongly suspected.”

“That means guessed.”

Danno climbed in first. The boat rocked so sharply he nearly fell out the other side.

Joeby pointed.

“That was not captain-like.”

“Get in.”

Joeby hesitated.

20

Behind them, Blackwater Cove was beginning to wake. Smoke rose from chimneys. A dog barked somewhere near the harbour. The church bell rang once.

“We can still go home,” Joeby said.

Danno looked at the village.

Then at the water beyond the harbour wall.

“We could.”

Joeby waited.

Danno smiled.

“Or we could see Pirate Rock.”

21

A wave nudged the dinghy away from the sand.

Joeby grabbed the side.

“Danno.”

“What?”

“It’s leaving.”

“Then get in.”

Joeby jumped.

The boat dipped, spun, and drifted backwards.

Both boys froze.

Then Joeby burst out laughing.

“We’re sailing!”

“We’re drifting.”

“That still counts.”

22

Danno grabbed an oar and pushed against the sand until the boat turned towards open water.

They passed the harbour wall just as the sun rose properly.

For a while, everything was perfect.

The sea sparkled.

The village shrank behind them.

Joeby ate an apple and declared himself first mate.

Danno said first mates didn’t eat all the breakfast.

23

Joeby said captains didn’t nearly fall overboard before leaving shore.

They argued happily until the cliffs of Blackwater Cove softened behind morning mist.

Then the wind stopped.

It did not fade.

It stopped.

The little sail they had tied from an old sheet hung limp against the mast.

Danno dipped an oar into the sea.

The water felt heavy.

He pulled.

24

The boat did not turn.

He pulled harder.

Still nothing.

Joeby leaned over the side.

“Are we stuck?”

“In the sea?”

“Could happen.”

“No.”

The dinghy drifted sideways.

Not towards Pirate Rock.

Not towards home.

Out.

Joeby looked back.

The harbour wall was smaller than before.

25

“Danno?”

“I know.”

“Are you rowing?”

“No.”

“Is the wind blowing?”

“No.”

“Then why are we moving?”

Danno had no answer.

The water around them had changed colour. It was darker now, deep blue streaked with silver, as though moonlight lay beneath the surface even in daylight.

The compass in Danno’s pocket began to twitch.

26

He pulled it out.

The needle spun.

Once.

Twice.

Faster.

Joeby stared.

“It’s broken.”

“It wasn’t.”

“Maybe the sea broke it.”

Danno snapped it shut, suddenly annoyed by the sound of the little needle ticking against the glass.

Hours passed.

The sun climbed.

The bread became dry in Joeby’s mouth.

27

The village disappeared completely.

At first they joked about it.

Then they didn’t.

By afternoon, the sea was everywhere.

No gulls.

No boats.

No land.

Just blue water and sky.

Joeby hugged his knees.

“Mum’s going to be furious.”

Danno stared at the horizon.

“She has to find us first.”

That was meant to be funny.

28

It wasn’t.

The mist came just before evening.

It rolled across the water in pale folds, swallowing the sun, the sea, and finally the boat itself.

Joeby whispered, “I don’t like this.”

Danno sat beside him.

“Me neither.”

That frightened Joeby more than anything.

Then the boat struck something.

Not hard.

Just a soft crunch.

Sand.

29

Both boys looked up.

The mist thinned.

A beach stretched before them.

White sand.

Black rocks.

Jungle rising behind it like a green wall.

Waterfalls flashed in the cliffs above.

Birds called from trees neither boy recognised.

Joeby swallowed.

“Danno?”

“Yeah?”

“I don’t think this is Pirate Rock.”

30

The little blue boat slid gently onto the shore of an island that had not been there a moment before.

31

CHAPTER THREE

The Island That Wasn’t There

For a while, neither boy moved.

The boat rested in shallow water. Tiny waves tapped its side. The beach ahead was empty, but not ordinary-empty. Not like Blackwater Cove before breakfast.

This was waiting-empty.

Joeby whispered, “Are we dead?”

32

Danno stepped into the water.

“No.”

“You checked quickly.”

“Dead people don’t have wet socks.”

Joeby looked down at his own feet.

“That’s true.”

He climbed out, nearly slipped, grabbed the boat and pretended he had meant to do it.

The air was warm and heavy with flowers. The jungle breathed strange green smells across the beach. Something bright red flashed between trees and vanished.

33

Joeby reached for Danno’s sleeve.

“What was that?”

“A bird.”

“Birds are not that big.”

“Big bird.”

“That is not comforting.”

Danno pulled the boat above the tide line. Joeby helped, though mostly by complaining.

“My arms are broken.”

“They’re not.”

“They feel broken.”

“That’s because they’ve done work.”

34

They tipped the dinghy on its side beneath a leaning rock. It made a poor shelter, but it was shade. Shade mattered. Thirst mattered more.

Joeby licked cracked lips.

“I’m hungry.”

“Water first,” Danno said.

“I’m also thirsty.”

“Then definitely water first.”

35

They followed the sound of trickling through ferns taller than Joeby. The jungle closed behind them quickly. Too quickly. After ten steps the beach had vanished.

Joeby stopped.

“We should leave marks.”

Danno looked back.

“That’s actually clever.”

“I have moments.”

They broke twigs and scratched arrows into soft bark until they found the stream.

36

It ran clear over black stones, cold enough to make their teeth hurt. They drank until their stomachs ached.

Joeby splashed water over his face.

“Best drink ever.”

“You say that about orange squash.”

“This is better island squash.”

Danno filled an old tin they had found in the boat.

Then he noticed the mud beside the stream.

Footprints.

Small.

Bare.

37

Not theirs.

He crouched.

Joeby leaned over his shoulder.

“Are those ours?”

“Your feet aren’t that small.”

“They used to be.”

“Not this morning.”

The footprints led away through the trees.

Joeby went very still.

“There’s someone here.”

Danno tightened his hand around the long branch he had picked up.

“Maybe.”

“Maybe?”

38

“Could be old.”

The wet mud glistened.

A drop of water slid into one print and filled the heel.

Joeby’s voice shrank.

“That doesn’t look old.”

A twig snapped somewhere in the jungle.

Both boys spun.

Nothing moved.

The trees stood thick and silent.

Too silent.

Danno lifted the branch.

“We’re going back to the beach.”

39

“For once,” Joeby whispered, “excellent plan.”

They moved quickly.

Not running.

Running would admit fear.

But not slowly either.

Back at the shelter, Danno sharpened the branch against a flat stone until it became a rough spear. Joeby watched, then chose a shorter piece of wood and began hacking at it with a shell.

“What are you doing?”

“Making a sword.”

“That’s a stick.”

40

“It’s a sword-stick.”

“You can’t fight anything with that.”

Joeby tied it around his waist with a vine.

“I look dangerous.”

“You look like a tree fell over and gave up.”

Joeby drew the wooden sword with great seriousness.

“Say that when I save your life.”

Danno laughed.

It felt good to laugh.

It didn’t last.

Night came fast on the island.

41

One moment the sky was gold.

The next, shadows poured from the jungle and swallowed the beach.

They made a small fire from dry grass and driftwood. Danno caught one tiny silver fish with his spear after missing six times. Joeby tried to catch one with his sword and soaked himself to the waist.

“That fish was scared,” he insisted.

“It escaped before you touched the water.”

“It sensed danger.”

“It sensed stupidity.”

42

They shared the fish in two bites each.

Joeby lay beneath the upturned boat, his shell clutched in one hand and his wooden sword under the other arm.

“Danno?”

“What?”

“If Mum finds us, are we grounded forever?”

Danno stared into the fire.

“Probably.”

“Good,” Joeby murmured. “That means she finds us.”

Danno said nothing.

43

The jungle began making noises after dark.

Whistles.

Clicks.

Long rustles that moved from tree to tree.

Once, something screamed so suddenly Joeby sat bolt upright and hit his head on the boat.

“What was that?”

“Bird,” Danno said.

“At night?”

“Night bird.”

“That was not a bird.”

“Then don’t ask.”

Joeby shuffled closer.

44

Danno stayed awake long after his brother slept. The spear rested across his knees. The fire sank into red embers.

Near midnight, the whistling started.

Not birds.

Not wind.

Three soft notes from the trees.

Danno held his breath.

The whistle came again.

Closer.

He stood slowly.

“Who’s there?”

Silence.

45

Then, at the edge of the firelight, two eyes blinked.

Small.

Bright.

Watching.

Danno gripped the spear.

The eyes vanished.

Morning arrived pale and damp.

Joeby woke first.

He yawned, crawled from beneath the boat, then froze.

“Danno.”

Danno sat up.

“What?”

Joeby pointed.

46

In the sand beside the dead fire were footprints.

Small, bare footprints.

They circled the camp once.

Then disappeared into the jungle.

Joeby looked at his brother.

Danno looked at the trees.

From somewhere deep inside the island came a sound like laughter.

Not human.

Not quite animal.

Then the jungle fell silent again.

47

Joeby drew his wooden sword.

“I don’t like this island.”

Danno picked up his spear.

He tried to smile.

But this time, even he couldn’t.

Neither boy noticed the girl watching from high in the branches.

Or the monkey beside her, smiling straight at them.

48

CHAPTER FOUR

The Hunt

Morning made the island look innocent again.

That annoyed Joeby.

Nothing that had whistled in the dark, left footprints beside your fire, and watched you from the trees should be allowed to look beautiful the next day.

But it did.

49

Sunlight poured through the palms. The sea glittered beyond the beach. Bright birds dipped and flashed above the stream. Somewhere nearby, something sang a tune that almost sounded cheerful.

Joeby did not trust it.

Danno, unfortunately, looked pleased with himself.

“We survived our first night,” he said.

Joeby sat beneath the upturned boat, arms folded, hair sticking up beneath his backwards cap.

50

“We nearly died of noises.”

“Noises can’t kill you.”

“You don’t know that. Some noises sounded very bitey.”

Danno laughed and poked the ashes of their dead fire with his spear.

“We need food.”

“We had food.”

“We had half a fish.”

“It was a very brave half a fish.”

“You ate most of it.”

“I was honouring it.”

Danno stood and looked towards the jungle.

Joeby followed his gaze.

51

“No.”

“You don’t know what I was going to say.”

“You were going to say jungle.”

“I was going to say breakfast.”

“In the jungle.”

“Probably.”

“No.”

Danno slung the spear over his shoulder and grinned.

“Stay here then.”

Joeby jumped up at once.

“That’s cheating.”

“What is?”

52

“Making me choose between the scary jungle and being alone on the scary beach.”

“You're welcome.”

Joeby grabbed his wooden sword from where it leaned against the boat.

The sword was really just a shaped stick with one end rubbed almost flat against a stone, but Joeby had tied it proudly to his waist with a strip of vine. He drew it now and held it out in front of him.

“I’m ready.”

Danno looked at it.

“For gardening?”

53

“For danger.”

“Try not to terrify the bananas.”

“There might be no bananas.”

“That would be the real danger.”

They followed the stream inland, keeping close enough to hear the sea behind them at first. Danno marked the path by cutting small notches into the bark of trees. Joeby marked it by complaining every few steps that all the trees looked exactly the same.

“They don’t,” Danno said.

54

“That one looks like that one.”

“It has red flowers.”

“So does that one.”

“That one bends left.”

“They all bend. They’re trees.”

The jungle grew thicker as they walked. Leaves brushed their shoulders. Vines hung like ropes. Insects hummed in the warm air. Every now and then, something small darted away before they could see it properly.

Joeby tried very hard not to keep looking over his shoulder.

He failed.

55

Danno noticed.

“You all right?”

“Yes.”

“You’re walking backwards.”

“I’m checking our rear.”

“Our rear?”

“That’s what soldiers say.”

“You are not a soldier.”

“I have a sword.”

“You have a stick.”

Joeby lifted his chin.

“All swords start somewhere.”

Danno smiled but said nothing.

56

That was one of the best things about Joeby. He could be scared of almost everything and still somehow keep walking.

They found fruit near the stream.

At least, Danno said it was fruit.

Joeby said it looked like something a frog would refuse.

It was yellow, soft, and grew in crooked clusters from low branches. Danno sniffed it. Joeby sniffed it. Neither of them died.

Danno took the smallest bite possible.

57

“Well?” Joeby asked.

Danno chewed.

“It’s good.”

“How good?”

“Better than half a fish.”

“That is not a high wall to climb.”

Joeby tried some and immediately reached for more.

“Fine,” he admitted. “Island frog fruit is excellent.”

They ate until their stomachs stopped hurting. Danno filled the old tin with more, and Joeby tucked two pieces into his pockets, one beside his shell.

58

“We should take some back,” Joeby said.

Danno raised an eyebrow.

“For who?”

“For later me.”

“Later you can climb the tree.”

“Later me might be tired.”

“You’re currently tired.”

“I’m preparing.”

A rustle sounded above them.

Both boys froze.

Something moved in the leaves.

Joeby slowly drew his wooden sword.

59

“Is it breakfast fighting back?”

A small brown face appeared upside down from a branch.

Round eyes.

Tiny hands.

A long tail curled around the wood.

The creature blinked at them.

Joeby blinked back.

“Monkey,” he whispered.

The monkey tilted its head.

Danno lowered his spear.

“Well, that’s new.”

60

The monkey dropped lightly onto a lower branch. It looked at Danno. Then Joeby. Then the fruit in Joeby’s hand.

Joeby held it protectively against his chest.

“No.”

The monkey smiled.

At least, Joeby was almost certain it smiled.

Then it sprang.

It landed on Joeby’s shoulder, snatched the fruit from his hand, bounced off Danno’s head, and vanished up the nearest tree in less than three seconds.

61

Joeby shouted.

Danno staggered backwards, clutching his hair.

The monkey sat on a high branch, eating Joeby’s fruit with both hands.

Joeby pointed his sword at it.

“That was mine!”

The monkey chattered.

Danno rubbed his head.

“I think he disagrees.”

“He stole my breakfast.”

“You stole it from a tree.”

“The tree wasn’t eating it.”

62

The monkey threw the peel.

It hit Danno on the forehead.

For one breath, everything went silent.

Then Joeby laughed so hard he dropped his sword.

Danno looked up slowly.

The monkey chattered again.

“I think,” Joeby said, wiping his eyes, “he likes you.”

Danno picked the peel from his hoodie.

“I’m going to throw him in the sea.”

63

The monkey seemed to understand enough to leap to another branch.

Joeby gasped.

“He’s getting away!”

“With your fruit?”

“With my honour!”

Before Danno could stop him, Joeby plunged after the monkey between the trees.

“Joeby!”

“I can see him!”

“Wait!”

“I’m winning!”

“You are chasing a monkey through a jungle!”

“He started it!”

64

Danno swore under his breath in the exact way he was not supposed to and ran after him.

The monkey led them brilliantly.

Too brilliantly.

It always stayed just ahead. Close enough for Joeby to think he could catch it. Far enough to be impossible. It swung over streams, bounced from branches, and once stopped long enough to pull a face that Joeby found deeply disrespectful.

They crashed through ferns and ducked beneath fallen trunks.

65

At first it was funny.

Then the jungle changed.

The stream noise faded.

The sea vanished completely.

The air grew still.

The monkey stopped chattering.

It crouched on a branch ahead, suddenly quiet.

Joeby slowed.

“Danno?”

“I know.”

The birds had stopped.

All of them.

66

One moment the jungle had been full of clicks, whistles and wings. Now it held its breath.

Danno reached Joeby and put one hand on his shoulder.

“Stay behind me.”

Joeby did not argue.

That was how Danno knew it was bad.

Something moved in the undergrowth ahead.

Heavy.

Slow.

Leaves trembled.

A branch cracked.

67

The monkey climbed higher, eyes fixed on the ferns.

Joeby whispered, “Is it another monkey?”

Danno tightened his grip on the spear.

“No.”

The bushes exploded.

A wild boar burst onto the path.

It was enormous.

68

Bigger than any pig Joeby had ever seen. Its black hide was scarred and caked in mud. Two curved tusks shone yellow-white beneath its snout. Its small dark eyes fixed on the boys with a rage that felt older than hunger.

Joeby stopped breathing.

Danno pushed him back.

“Move.”

Joeby stumbled behind him.

The boar scraped one hoof against the earth.

Danno raised the spear.

69

It looked suddenly ridiculous.

A sharp stick held by a boy.

The boar lowered its head.

“Danno,” Joeby whispered.

“Run when I say.”

“I’m not leaving you.”

“Joeby.”

“No.”

For the first time since they had left home, Danno sounded genuinely angry.

“Do what I say.”

The boar charged.

The ground shook.

70

Danno planted his feet.

He knew, with cold and terrible certainty, that the spear would not stop it.

But Joeby was behind him.

So he did not move.

The boar thundered closer.

Ten steps.

Five.

Three.

Something sang through the air.

Not a bird.

Not a whistle.

71

An arrow struck the earth directly in front of the boar.

The sound cracked like lightning.

The boar skidded, tusks ripping the soil. Another arrow landed beside its front hoof. Then a third struck the trunk of a tree so close to its ear that the beast squealed and twisted away.

It crashed back into the jungle, snapping branches as it fled.

Silence returned.

Danno still held the spear raised.

His hands were shaking.

72

Joeby clutched the back of Danno’s hoodie so tightly his knuckles had turned white.

The monkey dropped lightly onto a fallen log.

Then, from above, a girl spoke.

“That was brave.”

The boys looked up.

She stood barefoot on the thick branch of a leaning tree, one hand resting on a curved bow. Sunlight caught in her sandy-blonde hair. Her clothes were patched from leaves, cloth and leather, and a quiver of arrows hung across her back.

73

She looked about eleven or twelve.

Maybe older.

Maybe not.

It was hard to tell because she stood as though the whole jungle belonged to her.

The monkey scrambled up the tree and onto her shoulder.

She glanced at Danno’s spear.

Then at the gouged earth where the boar had nearly reached him.

Then back at Danno.

“Brave,” she said again.

A tiny smile touched her mouth.

74

“Very silly.”

Joeby let go of Danno’s hoodie.

Danno lowered the spear.

“Who are you?”

The girl crouched on the branch, perfectly balanced.

The monkey leaned forward and copied her expression.

“I was going to ask you that.”

“We asked first,” Joeby said.

Danno shot him a look.

The girl’s smile widened.

“I’m Derkik.”

75

The name seemed to settle into the trees.

The monkey chattered softly.

“And that,” she added, nodding to him, “is Lewis.”

Lewis bared his teeth in what might have been a grin.

Joeby stepped closer to Danno.

“Does he bite?”

Lewis leaned forward.

Derkik looked at the monkey.

“Only people he likes.”

Joeby swallowed.

“Does he like me?”

76

Lewis threw the stolen fruit peel at him.

It landed on Joeby’s cap.

Derkik laughed.

Danno stared at her.

“You live here?”

“Yes.”

“Alone?”

“Not exactly.”

“Are there others?”

Derkik’s smile disappeared.

For the first time, she looked past them towards the deeper jungle.

“Some.”

77

The word made the warm air feel colder.

Joeby tried again.

“Can you help us get home?”

Derkik looked at the boys.

Then towards the sky.

Then at the path the boar had torn through the undergrowth.

Her face changed in a way Danno did not understand.

“You shouldn’t have come here,” she said.

“We didn’t mean to,” Joeby replied quickly.

78

Derkik jumped from the branch and landed without a sound.

Lewis stayed on her shoulder, watching them with bright, clever eyes.

“Nobody ever does.”

Danno frowned.

“What does that mean?”

Derkik turned and began walking deeper into the jungle.

“Come on.”

Joeby looked at Danno.

Danno looked at the girl.

“Where?”

Derkik didn’t look back.

79

“Somewhere safer than here.”

Lewis did look back.

He smiled at Joeby as if to say the adventure had only just begun.
